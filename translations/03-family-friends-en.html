Key Phrases — Annotated
Block A · Immediate Family
mi madre / mi padre
my mother / my father
Never "mia madre" — possessives don't agree with gender in Spanish
"Mi madre se llama Ana y mi padre se llama Luis."
Block A · Siblings
mi hermano / mi hermana
my brother / my sister
Only the noun changes gender — "mi" stays the same for both
"No tengo hermanos. Tengo una hermana."
Block B · Extended
mis abuelos
my grandparents (both)
Masculine plural covers mixed groups: abuelos = grandfather + grandmother
"Mis abuelos están muy bien, gracias."
Block B · Extended
mis tíos
my uncles / uncle & aunt
Spanish uses masculine plural for mixed-gender groups
"Tengo dos tíos y tres tías."
Block C · Friends
mi mejor amigo/a
my best friend
"mejor" never changes — same for amigo and amiga
"Mi mejor amiga se llama Sara."
Block C · Relationships
novio / novia
boyfriend / girlfriend
(also: fiancé/fiancée)
Covers boyfriend, fiancé, and in some regions even spouse — context decides
"No tengo novio." / "Mi novio se llama Carlos."
Possessives — The Key Rule
mi singular → any gender my (one thing) mi madre · mi padre · mi hermano · mi hermana
mis plural → any gender my (multiple things) mis padres · mis hermanos · mis amigos
tu singular informal your (one thing) tu madre · tu padre · tu hermana
su his / her / their / formal his/her/their/your su madre · sus hijos · su mejor amiga
Common Mistakes — Avoid These
Traps
&
Fixes
Common A1 errors
mia madre
→
mi madre
Spanish possessives never agree with gender — "mi" is always "mi"
No tengo unos hermanos
→
No tengo hermanos
Drop the article after negation — "no tengo + bare noun"
¿Cuántos hermanas tienes?
→
¿Cuántas hermanas tienes?
cuántos (m) / cuántas (f) must match the noun — hermanas is feminine
Soy dos hermanos
→
Tengo dos hermanos
Use "tener" (to have) for family members — not "ser" (to be)
Real Dialogue — Block G
Sample Conversation · Block G1
Talking About
Your Family
A
¿Tienes hermanos?
Do you have siblings?
B
Sí, tengo un hermano. Se llama Carlos. ¿Y tú?
Yes, I have a brother. His name is Carlos. And you?
A
Yo no tengo hermanos, pero tengo dos primas.
I don't have siblings, but I have two female cousins.
Practice tip
Read aloud twice, then swap roles — this locks both question and answer patterns into memory at the same time.
Tips for This
Dialogue
¿Y tú? = "And you?" — bounce the question back naturally. Sounds very native.
pero = "but" — use it to add contrast. "No tengo X, pero tengo Y."
Se llama… = "His/her name is…" — works for any person you introduce.
Yo no tengo — "yo" (I) is optional but adds emphasis. Both forms are correct.
Cultural: "¿Están bien?" is a natural follow-up in family conversations — learn it early.
