Blocks C–H

Key Phrases with Annotations

Origin
Soy de México.
I am from Mexico.
Soy de + [country] — uses ser, not estar. Origin is permanent identity, so ser always. No article: "Soy de Canadá" not "Soy del Canadá".
Nationality
Soy mexicano.
I am Mexican.
No article! In English: "I am a Mexican." In Spanish: drop the article entirely. "Soy un mexicano" is wrong at A1 level.
Question
¿De dónde eres?
Where are you from?
Stress: DE DÓN-de E-res. The accent on dónde is mandatory — without it you're making a relative clause, not a question. The inverted ¿ signals the question opens.
Bounce-back
¿Y tú?
And you?
The single most useful conversation tool at A1. Always add it after your answer. It shows engagement and keeps the exchange alive. ¿Y usted? for formal situations.
Languages
Hablo español e inglés.
I speak Spanish and English.
Languages are always lowercase in Spanish. Note: e instead of y before words starting with "i/hi" — so español e inglés, not español y inglés.
Question
¿Qué idiomas hablas?
What languages do you speak?
Stress: i-DIO-mas — the DIO is stressed, not the first syllable. Idioma is masculine despite ending in -a: say el idioma, not la idioma.
Full intro
Me llamo Ana. Soy de España. Soy española. Hablo español y un poco de inglés.
My name is Ana. I'm from Spain. I'm Spanish. I speak Spanish and a little English.
The 4-part social intro: name + origin + nationality + languages. Rehearse until it flows as one unbroken utterance. This is the A1 conversational graduation test.
Tip
Hablo un poco de español.
I speak a little Spanish.
Immediately endears you to native speakers. Use it early and often. Un poco de + language = humble, warm, and instantly relatable.
Blocks D–F

Nationality Endings

The -o / -a Rule
mexicano→mexicana
peruano→peruana
cubano→cubana
venezolano→venezolana
The most productive pattern in Spanish. Nail it here and it transfers to every adjective.
The -és / -esa Rule
francés→francesa
inglés→inglesa
español→española
The accent on -és disappears in the feminine. "Francésa" is wrong — the feminine is francesa.

All Blocks

Key Tips & Traps

🚫
No Article with Nationalities
"Soy un mexicano" is wrong.
"Soy mexicano" — the article disappears entirely, unlike English ("I am a Mexican").
⚡
ser, not estar
Origin and nationality use ser always: "Soy de México." Estar is for temporary states. Origin is permanent identity.
✍
Languages are Lowercase
Write español, inglés, francés — not Español, Inglés. Spanish doesn't capitalize language names or nationality adjectives.
🎯
Americano vs. Estadounidense
Americano/a = anyone from the Americas (ambiguous). Estadounidense = from the USA specifically. Know the difference; use the right one in context.
📍
Accent on dónde
The accent on dónde is mandatory. Without it, you're not asking a question — you're starting a relative clause. This changes meaning entirely.
🔄
Accent Drops in Feminine -esa
francés → francesa (not francésa). The accent disappears when the feminine ending restores natural penultimate stress. Consistent rule — don't fight it.
