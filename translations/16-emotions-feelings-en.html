English Edition
Core Emotion Phrases
😊
estar + adjective
Estoy feliz
I am happy
"Feliz" never changes ending — same for men and women. Think: "fe-LEES."
😢
estar + adjective
Estoy triste
I am sad
"Triste" is invariable — no -a ending ever. It ends in -e, not -o.
😴
tener + noun
Tengo sueño
I am sleepy
"Sueño" = sleepiness (or dream). "Tener" because sleepiness is treated as a thing you "have."
😨
tener + noun
Tengo miedo
I am afraid
Fear is a noun in Spanish — you "have" it. Never: ~~estoy miedo~~.
😌
estar + adjective
Estoy tranquilo/a
I am calm
Think of English "tranquil" — nearly identical. -o for men, -a for women.
🤩
estar + adjective
Estoy emocionado/a
I am excited
Stress 4th syllable: e-mo-cio-NA-do. Changes to -a for women.
🍽️
tener + noun
Tengo hambre
I am hungry
"Hambre" is a noun — you "have hunger." Classic error: ~~Estoy hambre.~~
😠
estar + adjective
Estoy enfadado/a
I am angry
Chunk it: en-fa-DA-do. -a ending for women. In Latin America: "enojado/a."
😟
estar + adjective
Estoy preocupado/a
I am worried
Chunk it: preo-cu-PA-do. Stress on penultimate syllable, like enfadado.
🥱
estar + adjective
Estoy aburrido/a
I am bored
"Aburrido" = how you feel (bored), not what something is (boring). Different meaning!
🥶
tener + noun
Tengo frío
I am cold
Two syllables: frí-o. Never: ~~Estoy frío.~~ Frío/calor always use tener.
😊
estar + adjective
Estoy contento/a
I am pleased / content
"Contento" sounds warmer and more conversational than "feliz" in daily speech.
The Key Concept — Estar vs Tener

ESTAR

+ Adjective · changes gender
Estoy cansado/a → I am tired
Estoy feliz → I am happy
Estoy triste → I am sad
Estoy preocupado/a → I am worried
After estar, the word describes how you are — it's an adjective that agrees with your gender.

TENER

+ Noun · never changes
Tengo sueño → I am sleepy
Tengo hambre → I am hungry
Tengo miedo → I am afraid
Tengo frío / calor → I am cold / hot
After tener, the word is a thing (noun) — Spanish thinks of hunger, fear, and cold as things you "have." The noun never changes.

Empathetic Responses
🌿

To Good News

When someone says: "Estoy muy feliz / emocionado..."
  • ¡Qué bien! How great!
  • ¡Me alegra! I'm glad! (lit. "it gladdens me")
  • ¡Genial! Fantastic!
🍂

To Bad News / Struggles

When someone says: "Estoy muy triste / preocupado..."
  • Lo siento. I'm sorry / I feel for you.
  • ¡Vaya! Oh no! (European Sp.)
  • ¡Ánimo! Cheer up! / Come on!
  • No te preocupes. Don't worry.
Key Tips & Tricks
1
pronunciation

Stress the right syllable

emo-cio-NA-do · en-fa-DA-do · preo-cu-PA-do — all stress the penultimate syllable. Clap it out until it's automatic. "Muy" rhymes with English "mwee" — one tight syllable.

2
grammar trap

The Estar / Tener Switch

English says "I AM hungry / cold / sleepy." Spanish says "I HAVE hunger / cold / sleepiness." When your English sentence uses a physical sensation adjective, switch to tener + noun. Test: does it name a physical state? → tener.

3
register

Lo siento ≠ Perdón

"Lo siento" expresses empathy (I feel for you), not apology. Use it when a friend is sad. For actual apologies, use "perdón" or "disculpa." Using the wrong one can confuse your listener.
