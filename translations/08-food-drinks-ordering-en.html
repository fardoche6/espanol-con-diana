Tips & Tricks
El agua — why masculine?

Words beginning with a stressed a- sound take el (not la) to avoid the awkward vowel clash: el agua, el hacha, el alba. The noun is still feminine — adjectives go feminine: el agua fría.

Quiero is not rude

English speakers over-apologize. In Spain Quiero un café is perfectly natural — it's direct, not impolite. Add por favor at the end and you're golden.

La carta vs El menú

La carta = à la carte menu. El menú del día = fixed-price lunch (2 courses + drink, €10–15). Always ask ¿Tienen menú del día? for value.

Son cinco euros

Prices use son (plural) even for a single item — you're referring to the euros, not the item. Never say Es cinco euros at a till.

Pronunciation Traps
Ternera

Stress the second syllable: tair-NEH-rah. The R is a single tap, not a full trill here.

Quiero

KYEH-ro, not KEE-ay-ro. The qu in Spanish is always a hard K — no lip-rounding.

Cuenta vs Cuento

La cuenta = the bill. El cuento = a story. One vowel, very different situation at the cashier.

🇪🇸

Tapas culture: in Andalusia and the Basque Country, a free tapa often comes with every drink. Don't skip the drink hoping to just eat — the tapa is the bonus.

Tipping: rounding up to the nearest euro is the norm. 15–20% tips are unusual and can seem excessive.

Block G

Me gusta vs Me gustan — The #1 Trap

Singular noun → gusta
Me gusta el café
The coffee pleases me
Verb agrees with el café (singular)
Plural noun → gustan
Me gustan las gambas
Prawns please me
Verb agrees with las gambas (plural)

Paying the bill — Block H

La cuenta, por favor (The bill, please) · ¿Está incluido el servicio? (Is service included?) · Pago yo (I'll pay) · Vamos a dividir (Let's split it). Tipping: round up to the nearest euro — 15–20% is not the Spanish custom.
